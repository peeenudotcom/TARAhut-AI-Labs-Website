# Week 1: AI & Prompt Engineering Fundamentals

## Week Overview

**Theme:** Understanding AI and mastering the art of talking to AI
**Sessions:** 4 (Sessions 1-4)
**Total Hours:** 8 hours
**Key Outcome:** Students can write effective prompts and understand how AI "thinks"

---

## Session 1: How LLMs Work (Simplified for Teens)

**Date:** Week 1, Day 1
**Duration:** 120 minutes
**Topic:** Understanding Large Language Models -- How AI Actually Works
**Learning Objectives:**
1. Explain what an LLM is in simple terms
2. Understand the concept of "prediction" in AI
3. Differentiate between AI, Machine Learning, and LLMs
4. Identify 5 real-world uses of LLMs

### Materials Needed
- Projector with internet access
- Whiteboard and markers
- Student laptops with ChatGPT accounts (set up during session)
- Handout: "AI Vocabulary Sheet" (print 1 per student)
- Deck of playing cards (for prediction activity)

### Pre-Session Setup (Trainer)
- Test projector and internet connection
- Open ChatGPT on the projector screen
- Write on whiteboard: "Can a computer think?"
- Have playing cards ready for the prediction game
- Print vocabulary handouts

### Minute-by-Minute Session Plan

| Time | Duration | Activity | Trainer Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:05 | 5 min | **Warm-Up: "AI or Not AI?"** Show 5 examples on screen (autocomplete on phone, spell check, calculator, Netflix recommendations, Google Maps routes). Students vote: is this AI or not? | Use hand-raise voting. Accept all answers -- the goal is to spark curiosity. Most students will be surprised that autocomplete IS AI. |
| 0:05-0:15 | 10 min | **Recap & Introductions.** Since this is Session 1, use this time for introductions. Each student shares: name, class, school, and "one thing you have used AI for." Trainer introduces themselves and the course structure. | Keep introductions to 30 seconds each. If more than 15 students, do pair introductions instead. Set ground rules: no judgment, all questions welcome, phones on silent. |
| 0:15-0:20 | 5 min | **Instruction: What is AI?** Simple definition: "AI is software that can do tasks that normally need human intelligence." Show the hierarchy: AI > Machine Learning > Deep Learning > LLMs. Draw this as nested circles on the whiteboard. | Use the analogy: AI is like "sports" (broad category), ML is like "cricket" (specific type), LLMs are like "T20 cricket" (even more specific). Keep it relatable for Indian teens. |
| 0:20-0:30 | 10 min | **Instruction: The Prediction Game.** Take out a deck of cards. Lay down: 2, 4, 6, 8... Ask students: "What comes next?" Then lay down: A, B, C, D... "What comes next?" Explain: THIS is exactly how LLMs work. They predict the next word based on patterns. | This is the key "aha" moment. Emphasize: "ChatGPT doesn't think. It predicts. It has seen so much text that it can predict what word should come next, just like you predicted the next card." |
| 0:30-0:40 | 10 min | **Instruction: How ChatGPT Was Trained.** Explain the 3 steps simply: (1) Read the internet (pre-training), (2) Learned from human feedback (RLHF), (3) Now it predicts the best next word. Show a fun visual: "ChatGPT has read more books than every library in India combined." | Do NOT go deep into technical details. Keep it visual and relatable. Use the analogy: "If you read every textbook in your school, you could answer any question about any subject. ChatGPT read the ENTIRE internet." |
| 0:40-0:45 | 5 min | **Live Demo: See Prediction in Action.** Open ChatGPT on projector. Type "The capital of India is" and show how it predicts "New Delhi." Then type "Once upon a time, there was a" and show different completions. Ask students: "Why did it choose these words?" | Let students shout out predictions before ChatGPT responds. This makes it interactive and proves the "prediction" concept. |
| 0:45-1:00 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Account Setup.** Walk students through creating ChatGPT accounts (or logging in). Each student should successfully: (1) Open ChatGPT, (2) Type a message, (3) Get a response. Help students one by one. | Have a backup plan: if school email doesn't work, use personal email. If accounts get blocked, use Claude as alternative. Have printed step-by-step instructions ready. Pair tech-savvy students with those who need help. |
| 1:00-1:15 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: First Conversations.** Students try these prompts: (1) "Explain photosynthesis like I am 5 years old" (2) "Write a poem about cricket in Hindi" (3) "What is 2+2? Explain your reasoning." Discuss: How did AI respond? Was it correct? | Walk around the room. Ask students what surprised them. Point out when AI makes mistakes -- "See, it is predicting, not thinking! Sometimes predictions are wrong." |
| 1:15-1:20 | 5 min | **Break.** Students step away from screens. Quick stretch. | Remind students to drink water. No phone use during break -- encourage them to talk to each other about what they learned. |
| 1:20-1:40 | 20 min | **Independent Practice: "Stump the AI" Challenge.** Students work individually or in pairs to find questions that ChatGPT gets WRONG. Each student must find at least 3 incorrect or funny AI responses. They screenshot these for sharing. | This is always a hit with teens. They love finding AI mistakes. Common wins: recent sports scores, local Punjab facts, math word problems. Encourage creativity: "Ask about your school, your colony, your favorite local food." |
| 1:40-1:55 | 15 min | **Independent Practice: AI Vocabulary Journal.** Students fill out the "AI Vocabulary Sheet" handout with these terms: AI, Machine Learning, LLM, Prompt, ChatGPT, Claude, Training Data, Prediction. They write definitions in their own words + one example each. | Check that definitions are in the student's own words, not copied from AI. This journal will be used throughout the course. Students who finish early can add illustrations. |
| 1:55-2:00 | 5 min | **Wrap-Up: Exit Ticket.** Each student writes on a sticky note or says aloud: "One thing I learned" and "One question I still have." Preview Session 2: "Tomorrow we learn how to talk to AI so it gives you EXACTLY what you want." | Collect exit tickets. Use questions for Session 2 recap. End with energy: "You now know more about AI than 90% of adults in India. Seriously." |

### Homework / Take-Home Challenge
Ask ChatGPT to explain any topic from your current syllabus. Screenshot the conversation. Come prepared to share: Was the explanation good? What was missing?

## Session 2: Writing Effective Prompts

**Date:** Week 1, Day 2
**Duration:** 120 minutes
**Topic:** The Art and Science of Prompt Writing
**Learning Objectives:**
1. Write clear, specific prompts that get useful AI responses
2. Identify the difference between vague and specific prompts
3. Use the "5W" method for prompt writing
4. Improve a bad prompt into a great prompt

### Materials Needed
- Projector with internet access
- Student laptops with ChatGPT/Claude access
- Whiteboard and markers
- Handout: "Prompt Makeover Worksheet" (print 1 per student)
- Timer (phone or online timer on screen)

### Pre-Session Setup (Trainer)
- Write on whiteboard: "Garbage In = Garbage Out"
- Prepare 5 "bad prompt" examples on slides/whiteboard
- Have the "Prompt Makeover Worksheet" printed

### Minute-by-Minute Session Plan

| Time | Duration | Activity | Trainer Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:05 | 5 min | **Warm-Up: "Prompt Roulette."** Show a hilariously bad AI response on screen (e.g., ask ChatGPT "Tell me about cells" -- it might talk about phone cells, prison cells, or biology cells). Ask: "Why did AI get confused?" Answer: because the prompt was vague! | Get a laugh first. The funnier the bad response, the better. This sets up the entire session's theme: clarity matters. |
| 0:05-0:15 | 10 min | **Recap Session 1.** Quick quiz (verbal): (1) What does LLM stand for? (2) Does ChatGPT "think" or "predict"? (3) What was the funniest AI mistake you found in homework? Have 3-4 students share their homework screenshots. | Celebrate students who found creative AI mistakes. Address any questions from Session 1 exit tickets. Keep energy high. |
| 0:15-0:25 | 10 min | **Instruction: Why Prompts Matter.** Live demo -- same question, two ways. Bad prompt: "Tell me about Mughal Empire." Good prompt: "Explain the 5 most important achievements of the Mughal Empire in India, suitable for a Class 9 CBSE history student, in bullet points with dates." Compare the two responses side by side. | The contrast should be dramatic. Point to specific differences: length, relevance, format, accuracy. Ask students: "Which response would actually help you study?" |
| 0:25-0:40 | 15 min | **Instruction: The 5W Prompt Method.** Teach the framework: **Who** (who should AI pretend to be?), **What** (what do you want?), **Where** (what context/subject?), **When** (what format/length?), **Why** (what is the purpose?). Build a prompt live on the whiteboard using all 5Ws with student input. | Write the 5Ws on the whiteboard in big letters -- these stay up all session. Example build-together: Who = "Act as a Class 10 CBSE science teacher", What = "Explain Newton's 3 laws of motion", Where = "for a student who struggles with physics", When = "in simple Hindi-English mix, 200 words", Why = "so I can revise for my unit test." |
| 0:40-0:45 | 5 min | **Live Demo: 5W in Action.** Type the prompt the class built together into ChatGPT. Show the response. Ask: "Is this useful? What would you change?" Tweak one element live and show how the response changes. | Demonstrate that small changes in the prompt cause big changes in output. This builds the "prompt engineering" mindset. |
| 0:45-1:00 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Prompt Makeover Round 1.** Give students 5 bad prompts on the worksheet. They rewrite each using the 5W method, then test in ChatGPT. Bad prompts: (1) "Help with math" (2) "Write an essay" (3) "Tell me about India" (4) "Science question" (5) "History homework help." | Walk the room. Help students who are stuck. The most common mistake will be not being specific enough even in the "improved" version. Push them: "More specific! What chapter? What format? How long?" |
| 1:00-1:15 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Prompt Makeover Round 2.** Students pair up. Each pair takes their best "before and after" prompt and tests both versions in ChatGPT. They screenshot the comparison. Best transformation wins a small prize (sticker, extra point). | Encourage pairs to compete for the most dramatic improvement. Walk around and identify 2-3 great examples to show the class during wrap-up. |
| 1:15-1:20 | 5 min | **Break.** | Stretch, water, screen rest. |
| 1:20-1:40 | 20 min | **Independent Practice: "Prompt for Your Life" Challenge.** Students write prompts for 5 real scenarios from their own life: (1) Homework help for a specific subject (2) Explaining something to a younger sibling (3) Planning a birthday party (4) Writing a leave application for school (5) One free choice. They test all 5 in ChatGPT. | This makes it personal and relevant. Teens engage more when the task connects to their real life. Let them be creative with the free choice -- some will ask about games, movies, or social media. That is fine. |
| 1:40-1:55 | 15 min | **Independent Practice: Prompt Portfolio Page 1.** Students create a Google Doc or notebook page titled "My Prompt Portfolio." They save their 3 best prompts from today with: the prompt text, what 5W elements they used, and a rating of the AI response (1-5 stars). | This portfolio will grow over the 4 weeks and become part of their final assessment. Help students who need to set up Google Docs. Notebook is fine for students without laptop access at home. |
| 1:55-2:00 | 5 min | **Wrap-Up: Key Takeaway + Preview.** Ask 3 students to share their best prompt of the day. Trainer summarizes: "The better your prompt, the better AI works for you. You are the driver, AI is the car." Preview Session 3: "Tomorrow we learn FRAMEWORKS -- templates that make every prompt perfect." | Write the car analogy on the whiteboard for emphasis. Collect any remaining questions. |

### Homework / Take-Home Challenge
Write 3 prompts using the 5W method for any subject you are currently studying. Test them in ChatGPT. Bring screenshots of the prompts AND responses.

## Session 3: Prompt Frameworks for Students

**Date:** Week 1, Day 3
**Duration:** 120 minutes
**Topic:** Ready-to-Use Prompt Templates for Academic Success
**Learning Objectives:**
1. Apply 4 prompt frameworks to academic tasks
2. Create reusable prompt templates for different subjects
3. Use role-based prompting effectively
4. Build a personal prompt template library

### Materials Needed
- Projector with internet access
- Student laptops with ChatGPT/Claude access
- Whiteboard and markers
- Handout: "4 Frameworks Cheat Sheet" (print 1 per student)
- Handout: "Template Builder Worksheet" (print 1 per student)

### Pre-Session Setup (Trainer)
- Print both handouts
- Write the 4 framework names on the whiteboard (covered with paper -- reveal one at a time)
- Prepare 4 example prompts (one per framework) tested and ready to show

### Minute-by-Minute Session Plan

| Time | Duration | Activity | Trainer Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:05 | 5 min | **Warm-Up: "Guess the Framework."** Show 2 AI responses on screen -- one clearly from a role-based prompt ("As a science teacher...") and one generic. Students guess which used a framework and which did not. | Make it obvious so students feel confident. This primes them for the frameworks they are about to learn. |
| 0:05-0:15 | 10 min | **Recap Session 2.** Quick 5W review: pick a random student, give them a topic (e.g., "Mughal architecture"), they must build a 5W prompt on the spot. Class votes: good prompt or needs improvement? Do this 3 times with 3 students. | This is a fun, high-energy recap. Celebrate good prompts. Gently improve weak ones together as a class. |
| 0:15-0:22 | 7 min | **Instruction: Framework 1 -- ROLE.** Reveal on whiteboard: **R**ole, **O**utput, **L**ength, **E**xtra details. Example: "Role: You are a Class 10 CBSE math teacher. Output: Explain the quadratic formula. Length: 200 words. Extra: Use a real-life example involving cricket scores." Demo live in ChatGPT. | ROLE is the easiest framework. Start here to build confidence. The cricket example will get laughs and attention. |
| 0:22-0:29 | 7 min | **Instruction: Framework 2 -- RACE.** Reveal: **R**ole, **A**ction, **C**ontext, **E**xpectation. Example: "Role: Act as an ICSE English literature expert. Action: Analyze the poem 'Where the Mind is Without Fear.' Context: For a Class 10 student preparing for board exams. Expectation: Include themes, literary devices, and 3 possible exam questions." Demo live. | Compare RACE vs ROLE outputs side by side. Ask: "Which framework gave a more useful response for exam prep?" Students should see that RACE adds more structure. |
| 0:29-0:36 | 7 min | **Instruction: Framework 3 -- STAR.** Reveal: **S**ituation, **T**ask, **A**ction, **R**esult. Example: "Situation: I have a science fair next week. Task: I need to create a project on renewable energy. Action: Give me a step-by-step project plan. Result: The plan should be doable in 5 days with materials under INR 500." Demo live. | STAR is great for project planning. Teens relate to the "science fair panic" scenario. Show how AI becomes a project manager. |
| 0:36-0:40 | 4 min | **Instruction: Framework 4 -- Simple Student Framework.** Reveal: **Subject + Topic + Class Level + Format + Purpose.** Example: "Subject: Biology. Topic: Human digestive system. Class: 9 CBSE. Format: Mind map in text form. Purpose: Quick revision before unit test." Demo live. | This is the simplest and most practical for daily use. Tell students: "If you forget everything else, remember THIS framework. It works for any subject, any day." |
| 0:40-0:45 | 5 min | **Distribute Cheat Sheet.** Hand out the "4 Frameworks Cheat Sheet." Walk through it briefly. Students can refer to this during practice. | Give students 2 minutes to read silently. Then ask: "Which framework do you think you will use most? Why?" |
| 0:45-1:00 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Framework Drill.** Trainer gives 4 scenarios, one at a time. Students must pick the best framework AND write the prompt. Scenario 1: "You need help understanding a chapter." Scenario 2: "You want to prepare for a debate." Scenario 3: "You need a study plan for exams." Scenario 4: "You want to check if your essay is good." Students test each prompt in ChatGPT. | Circulate the room. Most students will default to ROLE because it is easiest. Gently push them to try different frameworks for different scenarios. The goal is flexibility. |
| 1:00-1:15 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Framework Remix.** Students take ONE topic from their current syllabus and write it using ALL 4 frameworks. They compare the 4 AI responses. Which framework gave the best result for that topic? They note their finding. | This is a powerful exercise. Students discover that different frameworks work better for different tasks. Some will find RACE best for exam prep, STAR best for projects, etc. |
| 1:15-1:20 | 5 min | **Break.** | Quick stretch. Encourage students to discuss with their neighbor which framework they liked best. |
| 1:20-1:45 | 25 min | **Independent Practice: Build Your Template Library.** Using the "Template Builder Worksheet," students create 6 reusable prompt templates: (1) Homework help template (2) Exam revision template (3) Essay writing template (4) Math problem-solving template (5) Science experiment template (6) One custom template for their favorite subject. | Students should write templates with blanks they can fill in later. Example: "Act as a [subject] teacher for Class [number]. Explain [topic] in [format]. Keep it [length]. Include [special request]." These templates become tools they use for the rest of the course. |
| 1:45-1:55 | 10 min | **Independent Practice: Template Testing.** Students pick their 2 best templates, fill in the blanks with real topics, and test them in ChatGPT. They rate the response and refine the template if needed. | Help students who are struggling with abstraction. Some 13-year-olds will find "template with blanks" tricky. For them, have them write complete prompts first, then identify which parts could be swapped out. |
| 1:55-2:00 | 5 min | **Wrap-Up: Framework Champion.** Quick poll: "Which framework is your favorite?" Tally on whiteboard. Share 1-2 best templates from the class. Preview Session 4: "Tomorrow we have a DEBATE: Can AI think like humans? Pick your side!" | The debate preview will generate excitement. Tell students to come prepared with arguments. End with energy. |

### Homework / Take-Home Challenge
Prepare arguments for tomorrow's debate: "Can AI think like humans?" Pick a side (YES or NO) and write 3 arguments. You may use AI to help research, but your arguments must be in your own words.

## Session 4: AI vs Human Thinking (Debate Activity)

**Date:** Week 1, Day 4
**Duration:** 120 minutes
**Topic:** Critical Thinking -- Can AI Replace Human Intelligence?
**Learning Objectives:**
1. Articulate arguments for and against AI "thinking"
2. Identify what AI can and cannot do compared to humans
3. Practice structured debate and public speaking
4. Develop a balanced, critical perspective on AI capabilities

### Materials Needed
- Projector with internet access
- Student laptops with ChatGPT/Claude access
- Whiteboard and markers (divide board into "AI CAN" and "AI CANNOT")
- Timer for debate rounds
- Debate scorecards (print 1 per student)
- Small prizes for winning debate team (optional: certificates, stickers)

### Pre-Session Setup (Trainer)
- Divide whiteboard into two columns: "AI CAN" and "AI CANNOT"
- Prepare debate rules on a slide
- Have 5 "challenge questions" ready for live AI testing
- Arrange seating in two groups facing each other (debate style)
- Print debate scorecards

### Minute-by-Minute Session Plan

| Time | Duration | Activity | Trainer Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:05 | 5 min | **Warm-Up: "AI vs Human Speed Round."** Trainer asks 5 rapid questions. For each, students guess who would do it better -- AI or human. Questions: (1) Summarize a textbook chapter (2) Write a love letter (3) Solve 100 math problems (4) Paint a beautiful painting (5) Comfort a crying friend. | No right answers yet -- this just gets opinions flowing. Students will disagree, which is perfect setup for the debate. Track the votes on the whiteboard for a fun visual. |
| 0:05-0:15 | 10 min | **Recap Week 1.** Quick review of all 3 sessions: LLMs predict words, good prompts matter, frameworks help structure prompts. Do a rapid-fire quiz: show a prompt and students shout out which framework it uses (ROLE, RACE, STAR, or Simple Student). 5 prompts, 1 minute each. | This consolidates the entire week's learning. Reward correct answers with verbal praise. Keep the energy competitive and fun. |
| 0:15-0:25 | 10 min | **Instruction: Setting Up the Debate.** Explain debate format. Divide class into 2 teams: Team AI ("AI can think like humans") and Team Human ("AI can never think like humans"). If students have strong preferences, let them choose; otherwise assign randomly. Explain rules: 3-minute opening statements per team, then rebuttals, then live AI challenge, then closing. | Frame this as a serious academic exercise. "This is the kind of discussion happening at IITs, at Google, at the United Nations. You are debating one of the biggest questions of our century." This elevates the activity in students' eyes. |
| 0:25-0:30 | 5 min | **Instruction: What Does "Thinking" Mean?** Brief trainer input: define "thinking" -- memory, reasoning, creativity, emotion, consciousness. Ask students: "Which of these can AI do?" Write responses on the whiteboard. This gives both teams ammunition. | Do not take sides. Present facts neutrally. Mention: AI can reason (sort of), has memory (context window), can be creative (sort of), but has no emotions and no consciousness. Let students form their own arguments from this. |
| 0:30-0:45 | 15 min | **Guided Practice: Team Preparation.** Teams huddle and prepare: (1) Opening statement (3 min) (2) 5 arguments for their position (3) 3 counterarguments against the other team (4) Select speakers. Trainer circulates between teams, helping both equally. | Help weaker teams with prompting questions: "What about emotions? What about creativity? Can AI be original or does it just remix?" Ensure every student contributes at least one argument. No one should be silent. |
| 0:45-0:50 | 5 min | **Guided Practice: Quick Rehearsal.** Each team does a 1-minute practice run of their opening statement within their group. Peers give feedback. | Check that opening statements are structured, not just "AI is cool" or "AI is dumb." Push for specific arguments. |
| 0:50-0:55 | 5 min | **Debate: Opening Statements -- Team AI.** Team AI presents their opening statement (3 min max). Other team listens silently. | Strict timing. If they go over, gently cut off. This teaches discipline. Other team should take notes for rebuttals. |
| 0:55-1:00 | 5 min | **Debate: Opening Statements -- Team Human.** Team Human presents their opening statement (3 min max). | Same rules. Encourage applause after each statement to maintain respect and sportsmanship. |
| 1:00-1:10 | 10 min | **Debate: Rebuttals.** 2 rounds of rebuttals, alternating teams. Each rebuttal is 2 minutes. Students must directly address arguments made by the other team. | This is where it gets exciting. Students will get passionate. Keep it respectful: "Attack the argument, not the person." If it gets heated, that means they care -- channel the energy positively. |
| 1:10-1:15 | 5 min | **Break.** | Let the debate energy settle. Students will keep arguing informally -- that is great. |
| 1:15-1:30 | 15 min | **Independent Practice: Live AI Challenge.** Both teams come together. Trainer presents 5 challenges that test AI vs human thinking. Students watch AI attempt each in real-time: (1) Write a poem about losing a cricket match (test creativity) (2) Solve a logic puzzle (test reasoning) (3) Give advice to a sad friend (test empathy) (4) Come up with a new business idea for Punjab (test originality) (5) Tell a joke that is actually funny (test humor). After each, class votes: Did AI pass or fail? | This is the highlight of the session. Students see AI's strengths AND weaknesses live. The empathy test and humor test usually expose AI's limitations clearly. Let students suggest additional challenges if time permits. |
| 1:30-1:40 | 10 min | **Independent Practice: Closing Statements.** Each team gets 3 minutes to prepare a closing statement incorporating what they learned from the Live AI Challenge. Then each team delivers a 2-minute closing statement. | Teams should update their position based on evidence. "We saw AI fail at humor but succeed at creativity. So our updated argument is..." This teaches evidence-based reasoning. |
| 1:40-1:50 | 10 min | **Independent Practice: Personal Reflection.** Each student writes a 1-paragraph reflection: "After today's debate, I believe AI [can/cannot] think like humans because..." They must give at least 2 reasons. This goes in their Prompt Portfolio. | Students can take a nuanced position: "AI can do X but not Y." Encourage complexity over simplistic answers. This is critical thinking in action. |
| 1:50-1:55 | 5 min | **Debate Results & Prizes.** Trainer tallies scorecards (based on argument quality, evidence, rebuttal strength). Announce winning team. Emphasize: "Both teams made great arguments. The real winner is everyone who now thinks more critically about AI." | Even if the scoring is informal, announcing a winner adds stakes and makes students proud. Give small prizes if available. |
| 1:55-2:00 | 5 min | **Wrap-Up: Week 1 Summary + Week 2 Preview.** Summarize Week 1: "You now understand how AI works, how to write great prompts, how to use frameworks, and how to think critically about AI. That is more than most adults know." Preview Week 2: "Next week, we use ALL of this to crush your board exams. AI becomes your study buddy." | End on a high note. Students should feel accomplished and excited for Week 2. Remind them to bring their textbooks/notes for Week 2. |

### Homework / Take-Home Challenge
Write a 1-page "My AI Manifesto" -- your personal rules for using AI. When will you use it? When will you NOT use it? What are the risks? What are the benefits? This becomes part of your Prompt Portfolio.
