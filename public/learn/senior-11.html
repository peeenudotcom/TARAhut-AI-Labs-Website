Peer Review Workshop 🤝

Giving great feedback is a skill. Learning to receive it gracefully is another. Both will help you your whole life.

💡

Why Peer Review?

Your brain is blind to its own mistakes — you know what you MEANT to say, so you don't notice what's missing. A fresh pair of eyes catches what you can't. The best companies in the world use peer review for everything — code, writing, design. You're learning a professional skill today.

The 3 Feedback Starters — Use these EXACT phrases:

❤️

"I like..." — The Strength

Start with something genuine you appreciate about their work. Specific is better than vague.

Example: "I like how you explained AI in simple words — I actually understood it!"
🤔

"What if you..." — The Suggestion

Offer an improvement as a question or idea, not a command. Keep it actionable and doable.

Example: "What if you added one real-life example here? It would make this section stronger."
✨

"This would be even better with..." — The Polish

A specific upgrade that could take it from good to great. Think about what the audience would love.

Example: "This would be even better with a visual/graph showing the data instead of just numbers."
⚠️

Feedback Rules

DO: Be specific, be kind, be honest, focus on the work (not the person), ask clarifying questions
DON'T: Say "it's great!" without explanation, criticise without suggesting improvement, compare to others' projects, be harsh or dismissive

🎯 Class Activity — Peer Review Round

Pair up with someone whose project is DIFFERENT from yours. Use the 3 feedback starters above to give them written feedback (at least 3 lines each). Then swap!

  • Review time: 5 minutes to read their work
  • Write feedback: 3 minutes using the starters
  • Share and discuss: 2 minutes each

The best version of your project isn't what YOU think is good — it's what your audience finds clear, interesting, and useful. Peer review helps you see through their eyes before the real presentation.

Presentation Coaching 🎤

A great project presented badly is a missed opportunity. Learn the structure that makes every presentation memorable.

🎭

The Golden Rule of Presenting

People don't remember facts. They remember feelings and stories. Your presentation should make the audience FEEL something — curious, inspired, surprised. Structure your story to create that feeling.

The 5-Part Presentation Structure — Click each stage!

→
PART 1 🪝

HOOK

Grab attention in the first 15 seconds

→
PART 2 ❓

PROBLEM

What problem does your project solve?

→
PART 3 💡

SOLUTION

How does your project solve it?

→
PART 4 🎬

DEMO

Show the actual work you built

PART 5 📢

CALL TO ACTION

What do you want the audience to do or remember?

⏱️

Timing Guide

Total time: 3–5 minutes max
Hook (15–30 sec) → Problem (30 sec) → Solution (1 min) → Demo (1–2 min) → Call to Action (30 sec)
Practise with a timer. If it goes over 5 minutes, cut something. Less is always more!

🎤 Rehearsal Mode

  • Set a 4-minute timer on your phone
  • Stand up and present your FULL project to yourself (or to a friend/family member)
  • Record yourself on your phone and watch it back — note 1 thing to improve
  • Repeat until it feels comfortable, not perfect
😬

About Nervousness

Here's the truth: every single presenter gets nervous, including experienced speakers. Nervousness means you care. Channel it into energy. Take a deep breath before you start. Speak slightly slower than you think you need to. Make eye contact with one friendly face in the room.

Preparing for Q&A

After your presentation, the audience and judges may ask questions. Don't panic!
Tips for Q&A:
• It's OK to say "That's a great question — I hadn't thought about that yet"
• Repeat the question before answering (gives you time to think)
• "I'd like to explore that in the future" is a valid answer
• Never make up an answer you don't know

🌟 Predict 3 Questions

Think about your project and write down 3 questions someone might ask about it. Prepare short, clear answers for each. This is how professional speakers prepare for Q&A!
