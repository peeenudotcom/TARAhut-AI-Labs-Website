Section 01

Why Prompts Matter

The same Claude. The same model. Wildly different outputs. The variable is you.

Two users ask Claude the same underlying question: "Write me a business email." One gets a generic three-paragraph template. The other gets a precisely targeted, persuasively structured email ready to send. Same AI. Same moment. The prompt is the entire difference.

The Quality Gap — Same Question, Different Prompts

❌ Weak Prompt

"Write me a business email."

Result: Generic template. Unknown recipient, tone, purpose, or goal. Requires 5 rounds of editing. Practically useless.

✅ CRISP Prompt

"You are a senior sales consultant. Write a follow-up email to a retail buyer who attended our product demo last Tuesday but hasn't responded. Goal: re-engage and propose a 30-min call. Tone: professional but warm. Length: under 150 words. Subject line included."

Result: Ready-to-send email. Correct tone, clear CTA, appropriate length. Maybe one small edit needed.

The Numbers Behind Prompt Quality

In our training cohorts, students with structured prompting frameworks produce outputs that require 60-70% fewer revision rounds compared to ad-hoc prompting. On a typical workday with 10 Claude interactions, that's 3-4 hours saved per week just from better prompt structure. Compounded over a year: 150+ hours.

What Claude Needs to Succeed

🎭

Role Clarity

Who is Claude being asked to be? A consultant? A copywriter? A developer? The role sets the entire frame for expertise, tone, and approach.

🎯

Goal Specificity

What is the actual desired outcome? Not "write a report" but "write a 3-page executive summary for a board that has 10 minutes." Specificity enables precision.

🔍

Context Richness

Who is the audience? What do they already know? What constraints exist? Context transforms generic output into targeted, situationally appropriate output.

📐

Format & Constraints

Length, tone, format, what to avoid — constraints are features, not limitations. They guide Claude toward exactly what you need, cutting everything else.

Section 02

The CRISP Framework

Five components. Every component earns its place. When all five are present, output quality becomes predictable.

📋

CRISP: The Components

C — Context  •  R — Role  •  I — Instructions  •  S — Specifics  •  P — Parameters

Not every prompt needs all five. But knowing when to use each component — and why — is the skill this section builds.

C

Context — The Situation & Background

Gives Claude the situational awareness it needs. Who is involved? What's the current state? What has already happened? Context answers "why am I doing this?" Good context prevents Claude from making assumptions that send the output in the wrong direction.

Without context: Claude writes for a generic audience
With context: Claude writes for your specific situation

Example: "I'm preparing for a quarterly review with our CEO, who is skeptical about our marketing ROI and prefers data-backed arguments..."
R

Role — Who Claude Is Being

Assigns Claude a persona, expertise level, and perspective. The role shapes vocabulary, depth, tone, and the assumptions Claude makes. A "senior investment banker" and a "friendly financial advisor" will write very differently about the same stock.

Without role: Claude uses its default helpful-assistant voice
With role: Claude brings specific domain expertise and perspective

Example: "You are a senior McKinsey consultant with 15 years of retail industry experience..."
I

Instructions — What to Do

The explicit task. Be specific, use action verbs, and break complex tasks into clear steps. "Write" is weak. "Write a 5-point executive summary that identifies the top risk, the key opportunity, and a recommended action" is strong.

Weak instructions: Vague verbs, multiple implicit assumptions
Strong instructions: Action verbs, numbered steps, clear deliverable

Example: "Analyze the following competitive landscape data. Identify the top 3 threats. For each threat, provide one strategic response. Use bullet points."
S

Specifics — The Details That Matter

The details that constrain and focus the output. Industry terminology, audience characteristics, specific examples to include or avoid, tone descriptors, required sections. Specifics are what transform a good output into the right output.

Without specifics: Claude makes reasonable but generic choices
With specifics: Claude targets your exact requirements

Example: "The audience is non-technical business owners. Avoid jargon. Include at least one analogy from everyday life. Reference Indian market conditions where relevant."
P

Parameters — The Constraints & Format

The output specifications: length, format, tone, what to exclude, response structure. Parameters are the guardrails that prevent Claude from going beyond what you need. They also signal that you've thought about the deliverable clearly — which itself improves output quality.

Without parameters: Claude defaults to its preferred length and format
With parameters: Output is immediately deployable

Example: "Under 300 words. Professional tone. No more than 3 sections. Include a subject line. End with a clear CTA. No filler phrases like 'I hope this email finds you well.'"
💡

When to Skip Components

For quick, simple tasks, you don't need all five. A quick factual question needs only Instructions. A simple edit needs Context + Instructions. The full CRISP framework earns its complexity for high-stakes, nuanced outputs where the gap between good and great matters. Learn to calibrate which components to include based on task complexity.

Section 03

CRISP in Action

Five real professional scenarios. See each CRISP component broken out, then assembled into the complete prompt.

Example 1: Business Email — Client Re-Engagement

C — Client is Rajesh Mehta, owner of a Ludhiana textile business. He attended our software demo 2 weeks ago, was interested, but has gone silent.

R — You are a senior B2B sales consultant who specializes in enterprise software for manufacturing.

I — Write a re-engagement email that creates gentle urgency, references a specific benefit from the demo, and proposes a next step.

S — He was most excited about the inventory automation feature. Q3 pricing ends in 10 days. He values directness over flattery.

P — Under 120 words. Professional tone. Include subject line. End with a specific ask: 20-min call Thursday or Friday.

Example 2: Research Report — Market Analysis

C — We're a Punjab-based startup considering entering the edtech space for Tier 2-3 city students in India. Pre-seed stage, team of 5.

R — You are a market research analyst who has covered the Indian edtech sector since 2019.

I — Provide a structured market overview covering: market size, key players, biggest gaps, and the most defensible niche for a bootstrapped entrant.

S — Focus on vernacular language content gaps, mobile-first delivery, and affordable pricing. Reference BYJU's, Unacademy, and PhysicsWallah as context.

P — 500-700 words. Use headers. Include a "Key Insight" callout at the end. Data-driven tone. Flag any assumptions clearly.

Example 3: Social Media — LinkedIn Post

C — I'm a business consultant sharing a lesson from a recent client engagement where a traditional family business nearly failed from ignoring digital tools.

R — You are a LinkedIn content strategist who writes in a direct, insight-forward style that generates meaningful engagement from business owners.

I — Write a LinkedIn post that opens with a hook, shares the story in 3-4 sentences, extracts a transferable lesson, and ends with a discussion question.

S — The core insight: the business survived by implementing WhatsApp Business and Razorpay, adding ₹40L in annual revenue. Target audience: traditional SMB owners considering digital transformation.

P — 200-250 words. Short paragraphs. No corporate jargon. 4-5 relevant hashtags at end. Hook must be in first 3 words.

Assembled CRISP Prompt — Business Email

Complete CRISP Prompt (Ready to Copy into Claude)

[Context: Client is Rajesh Mehta, textile business owner in Ludhiana who attended our ERP software demo 2 weeks ago and has since gone silent. He showed strong interest in our inventory automation module.]

[Role: You are a senior B2B SaaS sales consultant who specializes in serving manufacturing and retail businesses.]

[Instructions: Write a re-engagement email that references his specific interest, creates gentle urgency, and proposes a clear next step. Do not be pushy or desperate.]

[Specifics: He was most excited about reducing manual stock-taking time. Our Q3 pricing (₹15% discount) ends in 10 days. He values directness and practical value over flattery.]

[Parameters: Under 120 words. Professional but warm tone. Include a compelling subject line. End with a specific CTA: proposing a 20-minute call on Thursday or Friday this week.]
