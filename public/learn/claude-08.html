Claude for Deep Research

The 200K context window is not a specification — it's a capability that redefines what research means for professionals.

📚

The Context Window as Research Superpower

200,000 tokens is approximately 150,000 words — roughly 500 pages of text. This means you can upload an entire annual report, a complete industry survey, a lengthy legal document, or a PhD thesis, and ask Claude questions about it. Claude doesn't skim. It reads everything, holds it in context simultaneously, and answers with full document awareness. This is fundamentally different from summarization tools.

The previous standard for processing a 50-page industry report: 4-6 hours of reading, highlighting, note-taking, and analysis. With Claude: upload the PDF, ask targeted questions, get precise answers with page references in 20 minutes. That's not an exaggeration. That's what 200K context does.

What Claude Can Do With Long Documents

Extraction🔍

Find Specific Information

Ask for specific facts, figures, or claims from within a long document. Claude locates them precisely, with context — unlike keyword search which gives you the word but not the meaning.

Synthesis🔗

Cross-Reference & Connect

Ask Claude to find connections between different parts of a document, or contradictions between sections. This cross-document reasoning is where most analysts miss insights.

Analysis📊

Pattern Recognition

Ask Claude to identify recurring themes, patterns, or trends across a lengthy document. "What assumptions does this report consistently make?" reveals underlying biases most readers miss.

Summary📝

Structured Summaries

Not just a summary — a structured summary tailored to a specific audience. "Summarize this 40-page report for a non-technical board in 500 words" produces board-ready output.

Critique⚖️

Critical Evaluation

Ask Claude to critique the methodology, flag assumptions, or identify gaps. "What evidence is missing from the author's argument?" is a powerful research prompt.

Comparison↔️

Compare Multiple Sources

Upload multiple documents and ask Claude to compare positions, find agreements and disagreements, or create a comparative analysis. Multi-document reasoning at scale.

High-Value Research Prompts for Long Documents

Extraction Prompt

Finding Key Data Points

"Reading the uploaded report, extract all statistics and data points related to market growth. Present them as a bullet list with the context of each statistic and which section it appears in."
Analysis Prompt

Understanding Underlying Assumptions

"Based on the uploaded industry report, identify the 3-5 key assumptions the authors make about the market. For each assumption, quote the relevant section and evaluate whether the assumption is supported by the evidence presented."
Synthesis Prompt

Executive-Ready Summary

"Summarize the key findings of this 50-page report for a CFO with no time to read it. Format: 3 key findings, 3 risks, 2 recommended actions. Under 400 words. Business language, no academic jargon."
Critical Evaluation Prompt

Methodological Review

"Review the research methodology in this document. What are the limitations of the data collection approach? Are the sample sizes adequate? Are there any survivorship bias or selection bias concerns? Be specific with examples from the text."

Market Research

Three high-value market research prompt frameworks: competitive analysis, industry overview, and SWOT. Each produces structured, decision-ready outputs.

⚠️

Important: Claude's Knowledge Cutoff

Claude's knowledge extends to its training cutoff — not real-time. For current market data, pricing, or recent events, combine Claude's analytical capability with current sources: upload recent news articles, industry reports, or company announcements, then let Claude analyze them. Use Claude for reasoning and synthesis; use web search for current facts.

Competitive Analysis Framework

Competitive Analysis Prompt

5-Competitor Market Map

You are a market research analyst with expertise in [industry]. Conduct a structured competitive analysis for [your company/product] against the following competitors: [Competitor 1], [Competitor 2], [Competitor 3]. For each competitor, analyze: 1. Primary positioning and target customer 2. Pricing approach (premium/mid-market/budget) 3. Key strengths (max 3 bullets) 4. Key weaknesses (max 3 bullets) 5. Most significant differentiator End with a "White Space" section: What market position or customer need is currently underserved by all competitors? Base your analysis on the documents I've uploaded plus your training knowledge. Clearly flag which claims come from uploaded sources vs. general knowledge.
Industry Overview Prompt

Structured Industry Landscape

Provide a structured industry overview for [industry name] in India, covering: 1. Market Size & Growth: Current estimated size, growth rate, and 5-year trajectory 2. Key Drivers: Top 3 factors driving growth 3. Key Constraints: Top 3 factors limiting growth or creating friction 4. Major Players: Brief description of market leaders and their positioning 5. Emerging Trends: 3-5 trends reshaping the industry in the next 3 years 6. Regulatory Environment: Key regulations or policy factors affecting the market Format as a structured report with a one-paragraph executive summary at the top. Flag all statistics with "(based on training data)" or "(from uploaded source)" so I know the confidence level of each data point.
SWOT Analysis Prompt

Strategic SWOT for Decision-Making

Conduct a strategic SWOT analysis for [company/product/initiative] in the context of [specific market/scenario]. Constraints: - Each SWOT quadrant: maximum 5 points - Each point: one specific, actionable insight (not vague statements like "strong brand") - Under Threats and Weaknesses: for each item, suggest one mitigation approach - Under Opportunities: for each item, suggest one specific way to capture it - End with: Top 2 strategic priorities based on the SWOT Context to use: [paste any relevant context, financials, or market situation here] Format as a clean 4-quadrant table followed by the strategic priorities section.
🎯

Making Market Research Actionable

The failure mode of most market research is that it describes without prescribing. Always end research prompts with a "So What?" requirement: "End with 2-3 specific strategic implications for [your company/role]." This forces Claude to connect market data to decisions — which is the only reason market research exists.

Data Interpretation

Upload a spreadsheet or paste data. Ask Claude to analyze trends, create summaries, identify anomalies. No formula writing required.

📊

Claude's Data Capability

Claude can read, interpret, and reason about structured data — tables, CSV content, financial figures, survey results. It doesn't execute code in the standard interface (for that, use the API with code execution), but it can reason about data patterns, identify trends, flag anomalies, and explain what the numbers mean in business terms. This is often more valuable than raw computation.

How to Work with Data in Claude

Input Method 1

Paste CSV Content

Copy data from Excel/Google Sheets and paste it directly into Claude. Works for tables up to a few thousand rows. Claude parses the structure automatically and can reason about all columns.

Input Method 2

Upload CSV File

Upload a CSV file directly to Claude (up to 10MB). Best for datasets that are too large to paste. Claude reads the entire file and can reference specific rows, columns, and values.

Input Method 3

Paste Summary Statistics

For very large datasets, paste summary statistics (totals, averages, percentages, ranges). Claude can reason about patterns and implications even from pre-aggregated data.

High-Value Data Analysis Prompts

Trend Analysis Prompt

Monthly Sales Performance Review

I'm uploading 12 months of sales data [attach CSV]. Please analyze: 1. Overall trend: Is revenue growing, declining, or flat? Quantify the change. 2. Best and worst months: Identify the top 3 and bottom 3 months with possible explanations for variance. 3. Seasonality: Are there recurring patterns across quarters? 4. Product mix: Which categories are growing fastest and which are declining? 5. Red flags: Are there any anomalies or data points that require management attention? Format as an executive-ready analysis with a one-paragraph summary, followed by 5 numbered findings. End with 2 recommended actions based on the data.
Customer Analysis Prompt

Customer Cohort Insights

Here is our customer data for the last 6 months [paste or upload data]. Analyze: 1. Customer segments: What distinct segments exist based on purchase frequency and value? 2. Top customers: Who are our top 10% by revenue? What do they have in common? 3. Churn signals: Which customers haven't purchased in 60+ days who previously purchased regularly? 4. Upsell opportunities: Which customers show patterns suggesting they'd respond to premium tier offers? For each finding, provide one specific action the sales/marketing team should take. Keep the analysis practical, not academic.
Financial Summary Prompt

P&L Narrative for Non-Finance Audience

Here is our quarterly P&L data [paste data]. Please: 1. Write a plain English narrative explaining performance — as if you were briefing someone with no accounting background. 2. Identify the single most significant cost increase and evaluate whether it's concerning. 3. Calculate the key ratios: gross margin, operating margin, and revenue per employee (headcount: [X]). 4. Compare to last quarter if data is available and quantify the change. End with: "If I were the CFO, I would be most focused on [X] because [reason]." Use plain business language, not accounting jargon.
Survey/Feedback Analysis Prompt

Customer Survey Synthesis

I'm uploading survey responses from 150 customers [attach file]. Analyze: 1. Sentiment distribution: What percentage are positive, neutral, negative? 2. Top themes: What are the 5 most frequently mentioned topics or issues? 3. Most actionable complaints: Which complaints, if addressed, would have the highest impact on customer satisfaction? 4. Verbatim highlights: Select 5 quotes (positive and negative) that best represent the full response set. 5. NPS signal: Based on the language used, estimate an approximate NPS direction (improving/declining). Present findings as a brief report a product team would immediately act on.
⚠️

Important Limitations to Acknowledge

Claude reasons about data but doesn't execute statistical calculations with the same precision as dedicated tools like Excel, Python, or R. For critical financial analysis, always verify Claude's calculations manually or use proper analytical tools. Claude excels at interpretation and narrative around data — not as a replacement for statistical software.

The Research Workflow

A systematic, repeatable process: gather sources → upload to Project → systematic questioning → synthesize report. This is how professionals use Claude for research at scale.

🔄

Why Workflow Matters

Ad-hoc research with Claude produces ad-hoc results. A systematic workflow produces consistent, high-quality research outputs every time. This workflow is designed to be used as a template — adapt it to any research project without reinventing the process.

The 4-Phase Professional Research Workflow

1

Phase 1: Source Gathering

Before opening Claude, gather your sources: industry reports (NASSCOM, IBEF, World Bank), academic papers, company annual reports, news articles, survey data. Target: 5-10 high-quality, relevant sources. Download as PDFs or save as text files. Quality over quantity — 5 strong sources beat 20 weak ones.

2

Phase 2: Project Setup & Upload

Create a dedicated Claude Project for the research topic. Write custom instructions: "You are a research analyst helping me synthesize information on [topic]. When answering questions, cite the specific document you're drawing from. Distinguish clearly between information from uploaded sources and your general training knowledge. Always flag claims that would benefit from additional primary source verification."

3

Phase 3: Systematic Questioning

Don't start with synthesis. Start with systematic extraction. Run these questions sequentially:

→ "What do the uploaded documents say about [main topic]?"
→ "What are the key statistics and data points across all documents?"
→ "Where do the uploaded sources agree? Where do they conflict?"
→ "What does the evidence suggest about [specific question]?"
→ "What important questions are NOT answered by the current sources?"

Each question builds on the last. By the fourth question, Claude has processed all sources systematically.

4

Phase 4: Synthesis & Report

After systematic questioning, request the final synthesis: "Based on everything we've discussed and the uploaded sources, write a [format] research report on [topic]. Structure: [specify]. Audience: [describe]. Length: [specify]. Cite sources where possible. Include a limitations section noting what additional research would strengthen these conclusions."

The synthesis prompt comes last — after Claude has already processed and surfaced all the relevant material through systematic questioning.

Total Time: ~2 Hours for Professional-Grade Research

This workflow produces research that would have taken 2-3 days manually. The critical insight: most time savings come from systematic questioning (Phase 3), not from the final report generation. Knowing what to ask, in what sequence, is the professional skill this workflow teaches.
